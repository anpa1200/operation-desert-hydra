Executive Summary

This report provides an in-depth analysis of the activities of the Iranian cyber-
attack group MuddyWater within the Israeli cyber domain, focusing on their
tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs), tools, and primary attack targets.

The key topics covered in the report include:

   • Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs): A review of techniques used
     by the group, including targeted phishing, spoofing, persistence methods
     such as COM Hijacking, and the exploitation of communication protocols
     such as HTTP and DNS.
   • Campaigns: An overview of the group’s modus operandi, with a focus on
     the planning and execution phases of their attack operations based on
     specific objectives.
   • Tools: An analysis of the tools employed by the group, including the use
     of legitimate tools (LOTL) for malicious purposes (dual-use tools) and the
     development of custom-made tools, as well as the exploitation of
     protocols such as SOCKS5.
   • Infrastructures: Insights into the command and control (C2) frameworks
     used by the group, including Havoc C2 and Sad C2. The report also
     discusses the use of legitimate platforms (LOTS), such as cloud storage
     services and collaboration tools, which the group leverages to obscure its
     activities and distribute malicious files.


The report emphasizes the flexibility and innovation of the MuddyWater attack
group, which uniquely combines existing tools with custom solutions.
Understanding the group’s operational methods is crucial for developing
effective defenses against this threat.

Introduction
MuddyWater1 is an Iranian threat group operating under the Iranian Ministry of
Intelligence and Security (MOIS)2 since 2017. The group is also known by other
names in the cyber domain, such as Static Kitten, Zagros, and Mango Sandstorm.
The group primarily focuses on the Middle East, alongside operations in other
countries, including Israel, Turkey, Afghanistan, Pakistan, the United Arab
Emirates, Iraq, the United Kingdom, Azerbaijan, the United States, Egypt, and
Nigeria. This wide geographic distribution highlights the group’s global impact
and its capability to operate across complex arenas. Furthermore, its
international scope underscores the significant resources at its disposal and its
ability to execute sophisticated campaigns in diverse and dynamic operational
environments.


In the aftermath of the outbreak of the "Iron Swords" War, a marked increase in
cyber activity attributed to the MuddyWater group was observed within Israel.
This resurgence follows a period of relative inactivity, which may have been
influenced by the public exposure of the group's members at the CyberTech Tel
Aviv 2023 conference. The exposure, which was incorporated into a speech
delivered by the Head of the Israel National Cyber Directorate (INCD), heightened
awareness of the group’s operations and their potential implications.

Phishing Activity – Worldwide and in Israel
The threat group employs a range of phishing campaigns, both broad-based and
targeted (spear-phishing), across multiple countries to              infiltrate the
organizational networks of its victims. The identified attack methods include:

   1. Conventional Spear-Phishing: Creating accounts on free email platforms to
        send malicious links or attachments to carefully selected targets.

   2. Compromised Email Accounts: Gaining control of legitimate email
        accounts and leveraging active email threads to distribute malicious
        content, thereby making the emails appear as legitimate communications
        from the compromised account holder.

   3. Spoofing: Sending phishing emails from addresses designed to resemble
        those of reputable organizations, in an effort to deceive recipients into
        engaging with malicious content.



To enhance the credibility of their phishing campaigns, the group utilizes
advanced techniques, including:
   1. Impersonating Recognizable Entities: Constructing fake identities based
        on trusted organizations to enhance the perceived legitimacy of the
        communication.
   2. Distributing Emails from Compromised Organizations: Leveraging
        breached organizational email accounts to send phishing messages,
        utilizing content relevant to the organization's activities to increase the
        likelihood of success.
   3. Tailored Messaging: Moving beyond generic phishing templates, the group
        crafts personalized messages tailored to the victim’s specific area of
        operation.

   4. Exploiting Internal Organizational Infrastructure: Utilizing internal
        resources and infrastructure within compromised organizations to increase
        the authenticity and effectiveness of the campaigns.



These tactics underscore the group's reliance on sophisticated social engineering
strategies, designed to deceive victims in a targeted and customized manner in
order to gain access to sensitive resources.


In 2024, the group initiated large-scale spear-phishing campaigns across the
Middle East, with a particular emphasis on targets within Israel. These attack
vectors commonly involved links to reputable file-sharing services, such as
Egnyte and OneDrive, which were used to distribute compressed files. These
files often contained remote management tools (RMM), enabling attackers to
establish remote access to compromised systems. While these RMM tools are
legitimate and widely employed for professional purposes, they were exploited
by the attackers for malicious activities.


The use of RMM tools provides the attackers with full remote control over the
compromised devices, including the ability to transfer files, control input devices
such as the mouse and keyboard, monitor screens, and more. This level of access
allows the attackers to maintain a persistent presence within the organizational
network, facilitating subsequent actions such as deepening network infiltration,
collecting data, and preparing for further attacks.
For example, during one attack, MuddyWater used RMM to remotely exfiltrate
sensitive documents while simultaneously monitoring the victim’s screen activity
to gather further information about the organization. This level of control allowed

The group also occasionally conducts broad phishing campaigns that are not
specifically targeted at a particular nation or sector. These campaigns typically
involve impersonating legitimate companies to deceive victims into disclosing

In the most recent broad campaign identified, the INCD detected over 10,000
email accounts, including several Israeli accounts, that received an email
impersonating a legitimate software update from "Microsoft." In actuality, the
email contained a link designed to download and install a remote management
tool (RMM).


During the attack, the attacker utilized a dedicated API key embedded within the
email content, combined with the execution of PowerShell code. This granted the
attacker direct access to the agent after its installation on the endpoint. This
access enabled the attacker to control the compromised machine and continue
executing malicious activities remotely 4.

malicious tools as part of its phishing campaigns, alongside campaigns delivering
RMM tools. Some of these tools are modified versions of publicly available tools,
particularly those found on the GitHub platform. However, there is a clear
tendency for most tools to be independently developed by the threat group.

Technological Advancement and Expansion
An analysis of MuddyWater's activities over the past year reveals a significant
enhancement in its capabilities, reflected in its constant adaptation to the
evolving threat landscape and adoption of new technologies. These
developments are evident in the following:
1. In-House Development of Tools and Frameworks: The group demonstrates
    impressive adaptability by developing its own tools and frameworks. These
    innovations enable the group to overcome security measures and tailor its
    operations to various attack tactics, such as lateral movement within
    organizational networks and data exfiltration. A detailed review of these
    tools is provided later in the report.


2. Utilization of DLL Side-Loading (T1574/00236): This technique relies on the
    loading and execution of malicious DLLs by exploiting the system’s loading
    mechanisms. While it shares similarities with the DLL Search Order Hijacking
    technique (T1574/00147), it differs in its approach. Instead of passively
    planting malicious DLLs and waiting for them to be loaded by a user
    application via the DLL Search Order, DLL Side-Loading employs a proactive
    strategy.


    In this approach, the attacker installs an application—often legitimate—that
    serves as an intermediary for loading and executing the malicious DLLs. This
    process disguises malicious actions under the guise of legitimate application

Tradecraft
In 2024, the National Cyber Directorate identified several unique tools
developed by the group. Below is a detailed overview of some of these tools
detected in the cyber domain:


Bugsleep (PE64,C++)
The backdoor enables attackers to execute commands remotely on the
compromised system and establish a communication channel between the
infected machine and the C2 server. Once access is established, the backdoor
uses a scheduled task triggered every 43 minutes (customizable as needed) to
maintain continuous communication. The malware connects to a hardcoded IP
address and transmits sensitive information about the compromised system,
including the computer name and the victim’s username. This data undergoes
obfuscation to conceal its true nature and evade detection by security systems.
Key Functions of the Backdoor:
        1. Shellcode Injection:
           Injects malicious code into various legitimate processes on the victim’s
           system to mask its activity.
        2. File Exfiltration:
           Transfers files from the victim’s system to the C2 server, facilitating
           data collection and analysis.
        3. Remote Command Execution:
           Allows the attacker to execute commands on the victim’s endpoint for
           further malicious actions within the compromised system.
        4. Sleep Timer Updates:

Blackout (PE64, C++)
The backdoor operates as a Remote Administration Tool (RAT) and
communicates with the C2 server using the HTTP protocol. Communication
occurs through unique URLs and GET requests, which facilitate the transmission
of various commands between the C2 server and the compromised system.

   2. Submitting Execution Results to the C2 Server:

        POST /about-us
        Used to send the results of executed commands back to the C2 server.

   3. Uploading Files to the C2 Server:

        POST /awards/<filename>
        Transfers files from the victim’s system to the C2 server for analysis or
        further use.

   4. Downloading Files from the C2 Server:

process. This mechanism enables the server to present the correct certificate,
particularly in scenarios where multiple domains are hosted on the same IP
address and use HTTPS.

AnchorRat
A RAT that communicates with a C2 server and has the capabilities to upload,
download, and load various DLL files, depending on the command sent by the
operator.

Downloading and Loading DLLs via COM Hijacking:

COM Hijacking is a common attack technique where attackers exploit the
Component Object Model (COM) mechanisms in Windows operating systems to
gain control over the system, execute malicious code, and perform unauthorized
actions. COM is a legitimate mechanism that allows programs to communicate
with each other, but attackers can exploit the COM registry structure to replace
legitimate objects with malicious ones, thus hiding their activity.

By modifying registry entries, attackers can configure COM objects to point to
malicious files instead of the original ones. When legitimate software tries to
access a COM object, it might execute the malicious code instead of the
intended code. This allows the attacker to gain control over the system and
perform unauthorized actions.

The first command executed by AnchorRAT is downloading a DLL. To establish
persistence, the RAT uses the COM Hijacking technique by modifying a registry
key:
HKCU\Software\Classes\CLSID\{0358B920-0AC7-461F-98F4-
58E32CD89148}\InProcServer32.

The second command downloads and registers the RAT's executable file
(AnchorRAT) as a service named OneDriveStandaloneUpdaterService, creating

CannonRat
A remote-control tool that communicates with a C2 server to receive commands
and exchange information between the victim's computer and the server. In the
analysis of this version of the tool, it was found that it allows the attacker to run
various types of files, including DLL files, which may be used to execute
additional malicious code, install additional software components, or perform
further malicious activities on the victim's system.

Some of the commands supported by the RAT:

Sleep, pwd, cd, upload, download, runexe, rundll

Below is an overview of the main steps of the RAT's operation:

1. The RAT creates a folder named WinSys and copies itself to the path:
   %localappdata%\WinSys\Secure System.exe .
2. The tool writes a DLL file used as a loader to the following path:
   %localappdata%\WinSys\coreglobconfig.dll\ .
   This file is intended to load and execute additional malicious code on the
   victim's computer, thereby enabling the operator to expand access and
   control.
3. The RAT modifies the registry key:
   HKCU\Software\Classes\CLSID\{10D62541-90D0-42FE 848C-
   0DBC1AC42EDA}\InProcServer32
   to point to the DLL file it created (coreglobconfig.dll).
   The InProcServer32 key under the CLSID:
   {10D62541-90D0-42FE 848C-0DBC1AC42EDA}
   is used to establish persistence via COM hijacking.

   When a specific path to a DLL is set under InProcServer32 in the registry for
   a given CLSID, any access to this COM object or any action in Windows that
   loads this CLSID will automatically load the specified DLL (coreglobconfig.dll).
   This technique exploits Windows' DLL loading mechanism to execute
   malicious code. The loaded DLL performs the required actions (such as

4. The same DLL file (coreglobconfig.dll) re-executes the CannonRAT (Secure

Neshta
Neshta is an old malware variant that continues to be prevalent in the cyber
domain. It was first identified in 2003 and has previously been linked to the
BlackPOS malware. Neshta injects malicious code into infected files. This
malware often infiltrates organizational networks via unintended user
downloads or through other malware.

Neshta primarily targets Windows operating system files and can facilitate
attacks on network shares and removable storage devices (e.g., USB drives).
Over the years, the malware has undergone upgrades and modifications, leading
to a relatively large number of its variations.

As part of MuddyWater's use of legitimate tools with dual-use capabilities (both
legitimate and malicious), such as Venomand SimpleHelp, Neshta has been
observed as part of their operations. This integration allows the malware to
spread and infect additional files, leveraging legitimate tools as a disguise for
malicious activities. It aids in gaining control over the system or access to it
without raising suspicion.

Investigation Findings: re.exe

During an investigation held by the INCD, a file named re.exe was discovered,
which utilizes the legitimate SimpleHelp tool in the following manner:

   •    Downloader/Dropper Functionality:
        re.exe is a downloader/dropper communicating with a C2 server to
        download a JWrapper installation package containing the SimpleHelp
        remote control software.

   •    File Infection Mechanism:
        The malware infects all executable files that are not system files by adding
        malicious code to the beginning of the files while preserving their normal
        functionality. This ensures the malware spreads covertly within the

   •    Registry Modification:

        HKCR\exefile\shell\open\command\

Path and Execution of svchost.com:
The malicious file svchost.com is written to C:\Windows\ - It is used to run
the malware via the registry, executing the malicious code before launching
the original file requested by the user.

   •    Temporary File Creation:
        When an infected file is executed, a clean copy of the file is written to a
        temporary folder: C:\Users\<username>\AppData\Local\Temp\3582-
        490\
        The svchost.com file then runs the clean copy of the file to ensure the
        original software continues to function correctly. This behavior minimizes
        suspicion from the user while maintaining the malware's operation.

SOCKS5
During the research, several tools utilizing the SOCKS5 protocol were identified.
This protocol establishes a secure connection between the compromised system
and the attacker's server via a proxy connection. SOCKS5 helps bypass security
mechanisms, firewalls, and NAT, effectively hiding the connection's origin and
maintaining anonymity. Furthermore, the protocol allows data to be
transmitted covertly and efficiently, facilitating prolonged and effective attacks
while evading detection or blocking by security systems.

As part of the research, the attacker's use of the protocol was analyzed, leading
to the discovery of the tools service.exeand ego.exe, which exploit the protocol
and open-source libraries to create encrypted and secure connections. The
connection is established using an IP address and port provided as command-
line arguments. service.exe utilizes the open-source library "Gomux" after
establishing the connection, while ego.exe uses the "yamux" library. Both
libraries enable multiplexing, allowing the management of multiple bidirectional
streams over a single connection.

Details on ego.exe

The ego.exe tool is written in Go and packed with the UPX Packer to obfuscate
its purpose. It employs additional obfuscation techniques, such as removing
function names from the binary, making it difficult to analyze and understand
the executed functions. The tool also includes anti-debugging mechanisms to
hinder analysis and prevent detection of its malicious behavior during testing.

Additionally, the tool encrypts and decrypts data using the AES encryption
algorithm, ensuring the transmitted information is concealed and the

C2 Frameworks
As part of research conducted by the National Cyber Directorate, it was found
that some of the tools presented in this report interacted with server-side C2
frameworks used by the attacker as core infrastructure for operating malicious
tools. During the research, two new attack frameworks used by the attacker
were identified: Sad C2 and Havoc C2.

Sad C2
The framework supports the Pheonix and BlackPearl malware, as detailed later
in the report. The server-side component of the framework is written in Python
and provides the attacker with remote management and control capabilities
over the compromised systems, fully integrating with the malware. According
to initial analysis, the framework appears to have been independently
developed by the attacker, as no variations or similar sources were found in
public channels.

The framework utilizes FastAPI for communication, where the client initiates
interaction with the server. Subsequently, API requests are sent to perform
various actions, such as data retrieval, information updates, and execution of
additional functions based on the attacker's requirements.

The attacker, via the C2 server, communicates with the client side by sending
and receiving commands, as well as managing file transfers (uploads and
downloads). Communication between the client and the server under the
attacker's control is secured using symmetric AES encryption with the key
STAGER_AES_KEY. This encryption ensures that data transferred between the
compromised system and the attacker's server remains private, preventing
detection and neutralization by security systems.

BlackPearl (PE64, C++)

A remote access tool that provides the following functionalities:
        · Full control over the compromised system.
        · File transfer (upload and download).
        · Creation and management of shells.
        · Switching between communication protocols (HTTP and DNS).
        · Utilization of sleep timers to evade detection by security measures.

The payload creates a new Screensaver file named Bubbles.scr, which is
essentially    a    copy    of    the    TreasureBox       loader,      located     at:
C:\ProgramData\arch.1.65.com.microsoft\Bubbles.scr.
The specified path is passed to the Payload (Bubbles.scr) via a Pipe named
Archer, which serves as an inter-process communication channel.
Subsequently,      the   Loader   modifies     the   Registry   under     the     path:
HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Control Panel\Desktop, setting a reference to the newly
created file (Bubbles.scr). This ensures that the Loader is re-executed after a
system reboot.
Additionally, the RAT employs a synchronization/mutex object named Buta,
which ensures that only one instance of the RAT is running on the system at any
given time.

Summary of Findings and Investigation Report

MuddyWater has been active since 2017, and has undergone significant
evolution since its inception. Initially, the group focused on conducting targeted
cyberattacks in the Middle East. Over time, it expanded its operations and
became a significant player in both the Israeli and global cyber arenas.
One of MuddyWater’s most prominent characteristics is the continuous
development of its offensive toolkit. The group transitioned from using generic
tools to developing custom malware tailored specifically to its objectives.
Additionally, the group invests substantial resources in developing new methods
to evade detection systems, enhancing its efficiency and complicating efforts to
identify its activities.


While MuddyWater's operational focus remains centered in the Middle East, the
group is also active in other countries, including Israel, Turkey, Pakistan, the
United Kingdom, and the United States. In the past year, there has been a
notable increase in the group’s activity in Israel, particularly following significant
regional events, such as the "Iron Swords" War. This increase highlights the
group’s ability to exploit geopolitical events to expand its operations and
achieve the objectives of Iran’s supreme leadership.
